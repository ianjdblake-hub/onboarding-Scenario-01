Manager's Onboarding Scenario

Setting Up Jamie for Success at Meridian Corp

Your Challenge

You are Alex Rivera, Senior Director of Product Development at Meridian Corporation (5,000 employees). You've just hired Jamie Patterson as a Product Manager.

Jamie has strong skills but comes from a smaller company. Meridian's matrix organization and relationship-driven culture are new territory. New hires often struggle not from lack of ability, but from failing to build the right networks.

Your challenge: Balance giving Jamie autonomy (valued by company culture) versus providing structure to prevent costly mistakes. Your leadership effectiveness will be judged on how quickly Jamie becomes productive.

You'll make 5 key decisions over 90 days. Each choice has real consequences. There's no single "right" answer.

Decision Point 1: Week 1 - Setting the Onboarding Approach

The Situation

End of Jamie's first week. HR onboarding is complete. Your peer Marcus (Engineering) warns: "Make sure your new PM understands our culture first. The last outside PM burned bridges."

Your manager Patricia says: "Don't over-coddle them. People who succeed here are self-starters."

You have urgent deadlines. Jamie seems engaged but tentative. You meet in 30 minutes to discuss their plan.

Option A: The Structured Deep Dive

Create a detailed 60-day plan with scheduled 1-on-1s with 12 key stakeholders (you arrange). Prepare briefing docs. Jamie shadows meetings for a month before independent work.

Time investment: ~8 hours setup + ongoing coordination

Option B: The Strategic Starter Project

Assign a real project immediately‚Äîcompetitive analysis for product launch. Jamie must reach out to Sales, Customer Success, and Engineering independently. You make 2-3 key intros and have weekly check-ins.

Time investment: ~2 hours setup, weekly 30-min check-ins

Option C: The Buddy System Plus

Pair Jamie with Priya (successful 3-year PM) who can introduce them around. Jamie attends all your meetings for a month to observe. Assign smaller supportive tasks while they orient.

Time investment: ~1 hour to brief Priya, no extra meeting time

Option D: The Immersion Approach

Assign Jamie to the high-visibility Q4 product release team immediately. Weekly leadership reviews. They'll need to contribute quickly. You're in these meetings to observe and coach.

Time investment: Minimal extra (already in meetings)

Decision Point 2: Week 3 - First Assignment Strategy

The Situation

Three things just happened:

1) Patricia asks: "Haven't seen Jamie in product reviews yet. Are they getting ramped on deliverables?"

2) Urgent: Major client threatens to churn unless you deliver a feature integration in Q4. Needs a PM owner. High-stakes, high-visibility.

3) Annual planning starts in 2 weeks. Each PM presents strategy to VPs. Jamie's area (developer tools) hasn't had a PM for 2 months.

Option A: The High-Stakes Client Project

Jamie owns the enterprise client integration. Works with demanding Sales director, coordinates with Engineering, presents to execs weekly. You're closely involved but Jamie is the named owner.

Risk: High | Visibility: Very High | Your time: Significant

Option B: The Annual Planning Presentation

Jamie owns developing and presenting the developer tools roadmap. Three weeks to research, talk to stakeholders, and synthesize strategy. You review before final presentation.

Risk: Medium | Visibility: High | Your time: Moderate

Option C: Split Approach - Support Role on Both

Jamie supports you on client project (you own it) AND helps with planning background analysis (you present). Lower risk while building knowledge and relationships.

Risk: Low | Visibility: Low | Your time: Low

Option D: Continue Current Path

Extend current tasks‚Äîdeeper competitive analysis and top 10 customer feature requests with recommendations. Push client project and planning by 4-6 weeks. More relationship building time.

Risk: Very Low | Visibility: Low | Your time: Minimal

Decision Point 5: Week 12 - Performance Calibration

The Situation

Your VP asks for your 90-day assessment of Jamie. What do you say?

Option A: Advocate Strongly

"Jamie has strong potential and needs more time. The onboarding challenges are normal. I'm confident they'll be a strong contributor."

Option B: Balanced Assessment

"Jamie has clear strengths in [X] but gaps in [Y]. Here's my development plan for the next 90 days to address those gaps."

Option C: Performance Improvement Plan

"Jamie isn't meeting expectations. I recommend a formal PIP with specific goals. If not met in 60 days, we should consider other options."

Option D: Question the Fit

"I'm concerned this isn't the right fit. Jamie's strengths don't align with what we need. We should discuss transition options."
